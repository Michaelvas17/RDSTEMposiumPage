"But I must explain to you how all this mistaken idea of denouncing pleasure and praising pain was born and I will give you a complete account of the system, and expound the actual teachings of the great explorer of the truth, the master-builder of human happiness. No one rejects, dislikes, or avoids pleasure itself, because it is pleasure, but because those who do not know how to pursue pleasure rationally encounter consequences that are extremely painful. Nor again is there anyone who loves or pursues or desires to obtain pain of itself, because it is pain, but because occasionally circumstances occur in which toil and pain can procure him some great pleasure. To take a trivial example, which of us ever undertakes laborious physical exercise, except to obtain some advantage from it? But who has any right to find fault with a man who chooses to enjoy a pleasure that has no annoying consequences, or one who avoids a pain that produces no resultant pleasure?

On the other hand, we denounce with righteous indignation and dislike men who are so beguiled and demoralized by the charms of pleasure of the moment, so blinded by desire, that they cannot foresee the pain and trouble that are bound to ensue; and equal blame belongs to those who fail in their duty through weakness of will, which is the same as saying through shrinking from toil and pain. These cases are perfectly simple and easy to distinguish. In a free hour, when our power of choice is untrammelled and when nothing prevents our being able to do what we like best, every pleasure is to be welcomed and every pain avoided. But in certain circumstances and owing to the claims of duty or the obligations of business it will frequently occur that pleasures have to be repudiated and annoyances accepted. The wise man therefore always holds in these matters to this principle of selection: he rejects pleasures to secure other greater pleasures, or else he endures pains to avoid worse pains.

Device Security Survey of the River Dell Student Population

Research and Presentation by Michael Vasile and Gregory Sarafian (Sophomores)

Network-enabled devices, such as computers, smartphones, and tablets, have become a main-stay in many households. These devices have become deeply integrated into the lives of their users. In the age of frequent, sophisticated cyber security exploits and attacks, there is an increasing concern with the security of the devices, more so with the protection of minors who may use them. In this research study, we plan to develop a method to survey theses to find a trend in device vulnerabilities. We are aiming to survey the devices of members of the student body at River Dell High School. Further data analysis will be carried out to determine any trends in exploits and establish a relative benchmark for device security.

Sex-Specific Neural Activity

Presented by Noelle Almonte and Caroline Chan (Juniors)

Internalizing disorders, such as depression and anxiety, are characterized by internalization of negative emotions. It has been established that females are at a greater risk for internalizing disorders than males, but few studies have examined the neural mechanisms of such illness. A recent study conducted using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) examined the reaction times and differences in brain activation between different sexes and in both healthy and ill participants. Participants were shown a series of white arrows pointing either left or right on different sides of a cross-hair midline and were asked to press a button to report which direction the arrows were pointing in. Reaction times were found to be greater in ill females than in ill males, and females were found to deactivate portions of the default mode network (DMN) to a greater extent than males. Deactivations of DMN regions were also greater in ill females compared to ill males. Further research into the possible sex differences in the DMN may provide additional insight into the mechanisms of depression and the higher prevalence of depression in females.

Mapping Dark Matter Halos

Presented by Julian Munoz (Sophomore)

The Milky Way is a very large place, but it didn’t exactly begin as large as its current size. Galaxies eat and envelop one another through their gravitational pulls relative to their mass. But the majority of a galaxy’s mass does not come from regular matter, but rather halos of dark matter that surround a galaxy. Dark Matter is undetectable, however we do know it exists because of its gravitational pull on all things. When galaxies devour other galaxies, the galaxy being consumed leaves behind a stream of stars which all have similar properties such as metallicity . These stellar streams could not only help us investigate how galaxies initially began on an evolutionary scale, but also can give us insight on the shape of the dark matter halos that surround our own Milky Way.

Observation of Gravitational Waves from a Binary Black Hole Merger

Presented by Jacob Nam (Sophomore)

In 1916, Albert Einstein predicted the existence of gravitational waves in his general theory of relativity. Since the 1960s, numerous attempts have been made to experimentally detect gravitational waves. On September 14, 2015, gravitational waves were finally detected and were confirmed on February 12, 2016, about one hundred years after Einstein's prediction! Gravitational waves are ripples in the fabric of space-time, formed when large masses coalesce. They travel at the speed of light and distort whatever they travel through, including the earth. The Laser Interferometer Gravitational Waves Observatory (LIGO) detected this phenomenon, and this proof could lead to a completely new scientific field. This discovery is so significant because it allows us to observe the universe without the use of electromagnetic radiation, comparable to a deaf person receiving their sense of hearing. Black holes, not able to be seen, can be "felt" in this way, and we will be able to learn much more about them.

1.6V Nanogenerator for Mechanical Energy Harvesting Using PZT Nanofibers

Presented by Hahram Kim (Sophomore)

Piezoelectric nanowires, as demonstrated in their name, possess the remarkable capacity of generating an electric field through applied stress. Thus, piezoelectric nanowires and nanofibers are currently seen as a potential base for energy harvesting nanogenerators, carried out by converting mechanical energy to electrical. However, the recorded piezoelectric voltage constant of semiconductor piezoelectric nanowires in the most recent piezoelectric nanogenerator is inferior to that of nanomaterials constructed from lead zirconate titanate (PZT). These semiconductor piezoelectric nanofibers are also limited in the fact that the fabrication process of these semiconductor nanowires possesses drawbacks that could affect the length of these nanowires, cutting down on the potential electrical output of these nanogenerators. Thus, PZT-material based nanowires are currently the primary candidate for use in these nanogenerators. To overcome some of the limitations in semiconductor nanofibers, an incredibly efficient nanogenerator using PZT nanofibers on interdigitated electrodes is currently under testing in order to demonstrate its superior energy output and efficiency.

Hyper Dendritic Nanoporous Zinc Foam Anodes

Presented by William Lee (Sophomore)

Zinc has a rich history as an anode in a battery, but it has been overlooked by the far more popular lithium ion batteries because of its abundance and solid performance. Zinc has an undesirable trait; the formation of dendrites that leads to electrical short circuits. Conventional zinc battery systems that use composites, pastes and powders all have limitations. To try and solve this problem, researchers have tried to use a method of synthesizing zinc foam electrochemically. The zinc foam is formed by a three dimensional networks of dendrites that is electrochemically active and electronically conductive. This foam exploits dendrites and uses it to suppress initiations of dendrites during recharge. The battery has been tested and it has shown significant capacity retention after 100 charges and discharges.

Graphene Biosensors That Stick to Skin

Presented by Alyssa Kim (Sophomore)

In 2004, Graphene was first isolated using a simple but groundbreaking method: scotch tape. Graphene has become a phenomenon in the modern bioengineering world because its physical and chemical capacities- it’s one atom thick, 200 times stronger than steel, and conducts electricity, yet is biodegradable because graphite is carbon based- have proved to be useful in many biomedical applications, including wearable sensors. Biosensors is a general term used to describe sensors that detects specified biological components. In this study, Graphene-based wireless single cell bacteria detection on tooth enamel was done with single layered thin-film inductor-capacitor resonant circuit Graphene printed on silk and placed on tooth enamel, similar to how a temporary tattoo is applied. The large surface area of the Graphene and electrodes allows it to adhere to biomaterials, such as tooth enamel in this case. An external energy source is not needed because of the circuit, and data can be obtained remotely. Today, Graphene has become a valuable tool for scientists to advance in nanotechnology.

Biomedical Electrospinning

Presented by Chris Moon (Sophomore)

Tissue engineering, an actively growing field of biomedical engineering, is the study of the growth of new tissues from the use of scaffolds and cells. The scaffolds are made out of materials which have distinct characteristics, such as biodegradability and self-assembly. There are currently several methods that can be used to create these scaffolds along with a wide array of various materials that have been tested. One method in particular is Electrospinning. The technique was previously used in the 1900s to create fibers for clothing, but it has been growing in popularity due to its potential and versatility. The nanoscale fibrous mat is created by a polymer solution, which is shot out of a cone and a strong electric voltage is applied to it. The electrostatic repulsion, which is from the voltage, counteracts the surface tension and the drop is stretched out. The jet solidifies during flight and is deposited in the collector. The finished product closely resemble the matrix found in the body. As of now, researchers are experimenting with other materials, such as synthetic polymers and natural macromolecules, to create fibers with even smaller diameters.

PD-L1 & 2 Cancer Blockade Treatment for Cancer

Presented by Mary Ham (Senior)

PD-L1, 2 (Programmed Death Ligand 1 and 2) blockade is an emerging cancer treatment that harness anti-cancer immunity in cytotoxic T cells. However, PD-L1 blockade is not effective in treating all cancers. To identify factors that make Immuno-oncology more effective in treating cancer, it was hypothesized that Immuno-oncology is more effective in cancers that exhibit higher mutational burden.

Cancers that exhibit high mutational burden were identified based on genome analysis of various cancers, and the clinical trials of the selected cancers were analyzed through RECIST (Response Evaluation Criteria In Solid Tumors). Two types of cancers; non-small cell lung cancer and colorectal cancer with mismatch-deficiency were selected for analysis. As a control, the objective responses of ovarian cancer and colorectal cancer were also analyzed and compared with the objective response of Melanoma and colorectal cancer with mismatch-proficiency. Compared to ovarian cancer and colorectal cancer with mismatch-proficiency, non-small cell lung cancer and colorectal cancer exhibited higher percentage in objective response. Based on the data, the hypothesis was supported, but showed that the degree of mutation is not the only factor of effectiveness of Immuno-oncology.

How Our Universe Has Changed

Presented by Matthew Lu (Sophomore)

Cosmology is defined as the study of the nature, structure, and evolution of the universe. Throughout history, mankind has been on a quest to answer the fundamental questions of the universe: What is our purpose in life? What is in space? Are we alone in the universe? The list goes on and on. Great scientific minds like Newton, Einstein, and Hubble have vastly expanded our knowledge of the structure and laws of the universe. At the turn of the 20th century, it was believed that our understanding of the universe was more or less complete, and the few gaps in our understanding would be solved given time. But all of this changed with Einstein’s discovery of quantum mechanics and relativity. Although we have made significant strides in understanding in just the last century, our understanding of the way the universe works leaves much to be discovered, and huge questions remain unanswered.

Nano-wire Electrodes by Spin-Spray Layer-by-Layer Assembly

Presented by Brian Kim (Sophomore)

In order to produce fully functional transparent devices a more versatile battery structure is required. The current generation of transparent battery electrodes are thick (20-100µm) and are composed of optically absorbent material. By using a different, controllable method the fully functional battery is brought closer to reality. This method uses a rapid, and controlled spin spray layer-by-layer (SSLbL) method to create high quality 1D nanomaterial networks. This method helps create thin transparent layers of anions, something unseen in conventional methods.

How are Opioids Addictive?

Presented by Yussof Kazmi (Sophomore)

Prescription opioids are commonly used to treat patients with chronic nonmalignant pain. There is much controversy over the definition, incidence, and risk factors of prescription opiate abuse in chronic pain treatment. Such factors include environmental state and changes, social life, and physical injuries. While opiates/opioids may be used to treat pain in injuries if used excessively even the slightest bit can incur an addiction easily. Both chronic pain and prescription opioid abuse are prevalent and continue to exact a heavy toll on patients, physicians, and society. Individuals with chronic pain and co-occurring substance use disorders and/or mental health disorders, are at a higher risk for misuse of prescribed opioids.

Flexibility vs. Core Strength in Dancers

Presented by Evelyn Shats (Junior)

In order to test the correlation between strength and flexibility, a study will be conducted that records the core strength and hip flexibility of a number of dancers. These dancers will be between the ages of 12 and 18. The study will be tested on advanced female competitive dancers who train in ballet, jazz, contemporary, etc. A 60 degree ramp will be used to test the core strength. Dancers will lean on the ramp and then it will be taken away and the time that the dancers can hold the 60 degree shape will be recorded. The duration of time the dancers can hold a plank will also be recorded for figuring out their core strength. To test flexibility, the degree measure of the center split will be recorded. The hypothesis for this experiment is that the more flexible a dancer is, the less core strength they have. This study will be conducted in the next coming months.

Cost Effective Nanofiber Formation: Melt Electrospinning

Presented by Kevin Lee (Junior)

Electrospinning is currently the best and the most distinguished method of producing nanofibers in the field of tissue engineering. There has been relatively few studies and investigations of mel electrospinning than solution electrospinning in the past; however most solutions are expensive and extremely hazardous whereas melt electrospinning can directly produce the fibers while avoiding the use of solvents. Melt electrospinning is an easier and more efficient method of producing nanofibers than other general methods such as self-assembly and phase separation. In this presentation, I will present the basic understandings of melt electrospinning; the introduction to melt electrospinning and its process, the advantages and drawbacks of the technique, and the applications and impact of nanofiber production.

Utilizing Microsoft Kinect in the Classroom

Presented by Marlon Whitney (Senior)

The project I have been working is teaching through an alternate means that is meant to take a different approach compared to the traditional ways of teaching. Traditional ways of teaching involves visual and more auditory learning. Kinesthetic learning is a learning style where someone learns from using his/her body to help understand a subject. Visual learning is learning from what is shown. Auditory learning is learning through means of listening. This project is meant to teach students how to solve basic algebraic equations through kinesthetic and visual ways of learning. It accomplished this through 2 programs, one created by myself and an open source program, and the Microsoft Kinect. The Microsoft Kinect, with the aid of the other program, allows the student's whole body to become a mouse and interact with the program that I have created.
